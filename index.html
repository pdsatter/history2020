Wealth and Education Disparity

Wealth Disparity:

One major struggle America faces is the wealth gap between black and white households. The wealth gap was created after the abolition of slavery and continued to grow up until today. The reason for this continual growth is because after abolition African Americans were left stranded with little education and no experience living by themselves. This caused African American's to struggle right out of the gates. Their lack of education along with the systemic racism they faced made African American's the lower caste in America. They paid higher rents and recieve lower wages, which caused them to struggle to pay their bills. With just enough money to survive, often times black parents couldn't afford to send their kids to college and had little wealth left to pass down to the next generation. This lack of wealth occured generation after generation and continued to snowball. The rich get richer and the poor get poorer. In the book “The Warmth of Other Suns” Wilkerson shows that in Mississippi “White teachers and principals were making $630 a year, while the colored ones were paid a third of that - $215 a year” (Wilkerson 85). This problem existed over 70 years ago, however it still remains an issue today.

1968 - 2016:


Source: Washington Post

"In 1968, a typical middle-class black household had $6,674 in wealth compared with $70,786 for the typical middle-class white household, according to data from the historical Survey of Consumer Finances that has been adjusted for inflation. In 2016, the typical middle-class black household had $13,024 in wealth versus $149,703 for the median white household, an even larger gap in percentage terms." (Long and Dam).



This graph goes to show that the wealth gap between each race has actually increased in the past 40 years. In 1968 the wealth gap ratio was 1 : 10.61 in terms of black to white wealth. That means for every 1 dollar a middle class black household had, a middle class white household had about $10.61 of wealth. This ratio comes from a middle class black household having about $6,674 as compared to an average middle class white household with $70,786. However this ratio increased in 2016 where an average middle class white household had $149,704 in wealth compared to a black household with $13,024. This is a wealth depararity of 1 : 11.49 for black households to white. Despite 48 years of social progression happening, the wealth gap didn't decrease at all. Accounting for inflation it still increased.

How can we interpret this increasing wealth gap? Analyzing this graph, the most noticeable reason for the increasing gap is the growth in wealth between races. What do I mean by that? African American households wealth has minimally grown. Their wealth had an average increase of about $132.29 per year between 1968 and 2016. However if you look at the white households they have a much higher increase in wealth. On average a white household increases their wealth by $1,644.10 a year between the years 1968 and 2016. This is over ten times the amount of black households. Furthermore after the average wealth increased for the black population, it dropped back down during economic crisis. During the housing crisis African Americans took a large hit to their wealth, and have been recovering over the past decade. Now the Corona virus hits and black households are hit harder than anyone else, due to their economically weak state. Studies show that "more than 2 out of 5 black small businesses and self-employed workers have been forced to shutter during the pandemic" (Long and Dam). This focuses on the past 50 years in general, next the scope will narrow down to more recent years.

2013 - 2016


Source: US Federal Reserve
Note: The Washington Post's numbers are all lower than the federal reserve numbers shown here even though that was their source. This is because they accounted for inflation. So they are putting it in equal buying power as 1968, so the numbers are lower.

"Growth rates for the 2013-16 period were proportionally larger for Hispanic, other, and black families, rising between 30 and 50 percent, compared with white families, whose net worth rose 17 percent. Even with the large percentage gains for black and Hispanic families, the white-black gap in median net worth increased from $132,800 in 2013 to $153,500 in 2016, and the white-Hispanic gap increased from $132,200 in 2013 to $150,300 in 2016." (Dettling, Hsu, Jacobs, Moore, Thompson, and Llanes, "Recent Trends in Wealth-Holding by Race and Ethnicity: Evidence from the Survey of Consumer Finances")

The increasing wealth gap from 2013 - 2016 can be attributed a number of things. One is that during the great recession the wealth gap fell 30$ on average between everyone. However African American's had an additonal 20% drop after that. This means that they were hit harder in the recession than white families. Furthermore black households had a wealth growth of 30% and white households had a 17% wealth growth. While this may seem substantial this would still mean the wealth gap is increasing since the medium white household hold makes about 10 times what the mediun black household makes. So the increase of wealth between 2013 - 2016 would be about $24,846.15 for white house holds and $4,061.54 for black house holds making the wealth gap increase by about $20,784.61. *My caculations on these numbers are shown at end.

How can we interpret this data? One thing is for sure and that is that the wealth gap isn't going anywhere. It is actually only growing. The wealth gap in the 50's was obvious based on the more obvious signs of oppression such as segregation, unequal pay across the board, and even public lynchings. Now adays this monster is more hidden, as the signs aren't as obvious today as 70 years ago. However the wealth gap isn't something that is easily solved. With parents not being able to pass as much wealth onto their children, then this limits the growth of the children too. 1996 - 2010


Source: Washington Post

"Whites, Hispanics and Asians have all seen their college completion rates rise since 1996. Blacks have not... Only a third of black men who enter college graduate in six years — that is nearly half the rate of white men" (Long and Dam, "The black-white economic divide is as wide as it was in 1968")

"The median net worth of college graduates in 2016 was $397,100 for white families, but well below $100,000 for black families" (Dettling, Hsu, Jacobs, Moore, Thompson, and Llanes, "Recent Trends in Wealth-Holding by Race and Ethnicity: Evidence from the Survey of Consumer Finances")

These two sources pair to show the discrepency in education in America. Black students have graduated at approxiametely the same rate in the past 4 years while every other race has an increase of about 5-10%. WIth higher amounts of debt, yet less people graduating college creates even more debt, with even less wealth generated back from that debt.
